## 祖母的火塘 / Grandmother's Hearth

The Mosuo people of Lugu Lake are one of the last matrilineal societies in the world. Their architecture is organised around the *zhumu fang* — the grandmother's room — and at its centre, the *fire pit*, which burns without interruption from birth to death. It is simultaneously kitchen, altar, and social court.

This project asks: as young Mosuo leave for cities and tourism transforms the economy, what happens to this architecture when the grandmother is gone?

![Lugu Lake landscape](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1502082553048-f009c37129b9?auto=format&fit=crop&w=1400&q=80)

## The Tension

The project does not romanticise preservation. The Mosuo themselves are not a museum exhibit — they want education, income, connectivity. The question is whether modernisation can happen *through* the spatial logic of the existing culture rather than *against* it.

We identified three recurring tensions:

- **Open vs. private** — traditional Mosuo courtyards blur boundaries in ways that conflict with tourism's demand for separation
- **Continuity vs. legibility** — fire pits don't make sense to visitors; but explaining them strips them of meaning
- **Women's authority vs. male-dominated construction industry** — the physical building process itself can undermine the culture it aims to preserve

## Soft Balance

Our intervention strategy is one of *adjacency rather than insertion*. New programme — a community gathering space and small-scale homestay — is placed in satellite relationship to existing *zhumu fang* structures, connected by covered thresholds that mediate between tourist movement and household privacy.

> We don't touch the fire pit. We build around it, in a way that acknowledges it is the centre without making it a spectacle.

The material choices are deliberately local: rammed earth, pine timber, and woven bamboo screens — all available within the region and all maintainable by local hands. The project is currently in research and design development phase.
