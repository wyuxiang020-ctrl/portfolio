## Brief

Nelson, a post-industrial town in Lancashire, is undergoing significant transformation as its former commercial centre faces demolition. The gap between clearance and redevelopment creates a void — not just physical, but social. *Hard Hat Café* is a response to this in-between condition: a lightweight, moveable community café that activates the transitional space and gives residents a stake in what comes next.

The project emerged from a live brief set by MSA Live in collaboration with In-Situ, a local arts organisation deeply embedded in the community. Working in a 12-person cross-year team, we developed a proposal that could be assembled, disassembled, and relocated as the demolition progresses.

## Concept

The café operates as a social anchor during uncertainty. Rather than treating the demolition as pure loss, the design frames it as a stage — the construction barriers become part of the installation, the hard hat becomes the emblem of a community in transition.

Three principles guided the design:

- **Modular** — each unit can be transported in a standard van and assembled by two people in under four hours
- **Legible** — the structure reads as clearly temporary, without apology
- **Generous** — covered seating, power points, a small kitchen, and flexible indoor/outdoor use

## Process

Working across year groups meant negotiating different design instincts and technical knowledge levels. I contributed primarily to the structural module design and the material language — particularly the use of scaffold board, powder-coated steel, and printed wayfinding elements.

The project was developed through weekly community consultations, rapid prototyping, and close coordination with In-Situ's programme calendar. Each iteration was tested against two criteria: *Can a non-specialist build it?* and *Does it feel worth staying in?*

> A good temporary building doesn't pretend to be permanent. It arrives honestly, does its work, and leaves no scar.
