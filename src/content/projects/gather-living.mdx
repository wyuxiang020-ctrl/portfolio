## "20+" — A Generation Without a Fixed Address

In today's rapidly changing urban context, what young people need is not necessarily to buy a house, but an economically suitable "flexible" home that can facilitate their development in urban work and life. They can live here without any burden, devote themselves to their community, live in convenience, enjoy fashion information, experience fitness and entertainment — a shared home for physical and mental restoration, and a "shelter" for the struggling young people in the city.

*Gather Living* is a youth housing proposal for Chongqing's University Town: a district of 14 universities and approximately 200,000 residents in the Shapingba district — the western region's centre of advanced personnel training and international science and technology education. The site sits adjacent to Sichuan Fine Arts Institute, near Xijie Commercial Street to the north and the University Town Central Park to the south.

## Three Residents, Three Demands

Research identified three distinct user groups, each with different spatial needs and different relationships to community:

**Live Alone** — students and young professionals in the early career stage, with high social needs, frequent use of public facilities, and reliance on fast food. Units range from 35–78 ㎡, located on lower floors close to shared amenities.

**Joint Rent** — peers sharing for cost and connection, seeking quiet focus alongside social exchange. Units range from 50–80 ㎡, distributed in the mid-levels for relative calm.

**Family** — couples with or without children, requiring greater privacy and child-oriented space. Units range from 120–145 ㎡, positioned in the western portion of the block.

Each group also carries distinct secondary needs: learning space, co-working space, pet interaction areas, community canteen, social terraces, viewing decks, exercise facilities, and parent-child playgrounds. The design maps these needs spatially, ensuring no group uses the same zone as their primary route.

## Three Strategies

The design is structured around three interlocking strategies:

**Modularisation** — A 3.6 m × 3.6 m base unit generates all apartment types through combination. The module can be assembled, stacked, reconfigured, and transported — giving the housing a lifespan that outlasts any single occupant's tenure.

**Shared Concept** — Co-living and co-working are not afterthoughts but structural elements. Co-working + co-playing spaces are woven into each floor plate, making social exchange not just possible but architecturally inevitable.

**Sustainable Concept** — Prefabricated steel frames, factory-produced panels, and a dry-assembly construction process reduce waste and on-site labour. The system is designed for future adaptation: units can be subdivided, combined, or reprogrammed as demographics shift.

## Seven Unit Types

The modular base generates a family of seven distinct unit configurations:

| Type | Configuration | Area | Users |
|------|--------------|------|-------|
| A1 Single | 1 bed, 1 living | 39 ㎡ | Singles, limited budget |
| A2 Single | 2 bed, 1 living | 65 ㎡ | Singles, comfortable budget |
| A3 Shared | 3 bed, 1 living | 104 ㎡ | 2–3 person share |
| B1 Shared | 2 bed, 1 living | 52 ㎡ | Single or couple |
| B2 Shared | 3 bed, 1 living | 78–104 ㎡ | Couple or triple share |
| C1 Family | 4 bed, 1 living | 143 ㎡ | Family with children |
| C2 Family | 4 bed, 1 living | 143 ㎡ | Family with children |

> Young people don't need a house. They need a place that gives them room to become who they are going to be.

## Modular Construction System

The assembly sequence follows six steps — transportation of factory-made components, site measurement, prefabricated steel frame foundation, column mounting (100 mm × 100 mm steel column fixed with angle brackets and bolts), prefabricated panel installation (windows, doors, walls), and final module connection. The result is a building that can be assembled by a small team without specialist machinery, and adapted or relocated as needs change.
